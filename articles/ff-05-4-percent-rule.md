In 1994, a financial researcher named William Bengen published a paper that quietly became the mathematical foundation of financial freedom for millions of people.

Most people who cite it have never read it.

The paper was called "Determining Withdrawal Rates Using Historical Data." It was published in the *Journal of Financial Planning.* It answered a question that seems simple but had never been rigorously studied: how much of your portfolio can you withdraw each year, adjusted for inflation, without running out of money over a 30-year retirement?

Bengen's answer, based on historical US market data going back to 1926, was 4.15%. He rounded it down to 4% as a margin of safety. The term "safe withdrawal rate" was born.

Four years later, three professors at Trinity University — Philip Cooley, Carl Hubbard, and Daniel Walz — confirmed Bengen's work with a broader study of rolling 30-year retirement periods from 1926 to 1995. Their conclusion: a 4% withdrawal rate on a portfolio of 50% stocks and 50% bonds had a **historical success rate of approximately 95%** over any 30-year period in their dataset.

That is the Trinity Study. That is the 4% rule. That is what "95% of the time, you will not run out of money" actually means.

---

## What It Means in Practice

The 4% rule produces one of the most useful calculations in personal finance: your financial independence number.

Take your annual expenses. Multiply by 25. The result is the portfolio you need to be financially free.

If you spend $40,000 per year: your number is **$1,000,000.**
If you spend $60,000 per year: your number is **$1,500,000.**
If you spend $80,000 per year: your number is **$2,000,000.**
If you spend $100,000 per year: your number is **$2,500,000.**

At your number, invested in a diversified portfolio of low-cost index funds, historical data suggests you can withdraw your annual expenses every year — adjusted for inflation — for 30 years, and have a 95% chance of never running out.

The remaining 5% of historical scenarios? Those were periods when someone retired right before a major sustained market downturn — the Great Depression, the stagflation of the 1970s — and withdrew heavily during the worst years. Even in those scenarios, some portfolios survived. But not all.

## What People Get Wrong About the 4% Rule

**Mistake one: treating 95% as a guarantee.**

It is not. It is a historical probability. Past markets are not a binding contract about future markets. The 4% rule is a well-reasoned starting point, not a promise.

**Mistake two: applying a 30-year rule to a 50-year retirement.**

The Trinity Study modeled 30-year retirements. If you retire at 40 rather than 65, you might need your money to last 50 years or more. The historical success rate of 4% over 50 years drops meaningfully — to around 80 to 85%. For a longer retirement horizon, most serious researchers now recommend 3.5% or even 3.25% as a safer withdrawal rate.

William Bengen himself, in 2025 updates to his original work, raised his SAFEMAX figure to **4.7%** with broader asset diversification — showing that international diversification and small-cap allocation can improve the outcome. Meanwhile, Morningstar's forward-looking estimate for today's starting valuations is **3.9%**, slightly below the original 4%.

Wade Pfau and Karsten Jeske — two of the most rigorous researchers in retirement planning — recommend **3.0 to 3.5%** for early retirees with long time horizons.

**Mistake three: ignoring spending flexibility.**

The 4% rule models a rigid annual withdrawal that increases with inflation, regardless of market conditions. Real people do not spend like that. Most people with financial independence spend less in bad market years and more in good ones. This flexibility dramatically improves real-world outcomes beyond what the model shows.

**Mistake four: forgetting the other side of the equation.**

The 4% rule is about withdrawal. The spending side is equally important. People who retire with $2 million and spend $100,000 per year are using a 5% withdrawal rate — meaningfully above the safe zone. People who retire with $2 million and spend $60,000 are at 3% — comfortably conservative. The number you need is determined by your spending, not just your portfolio size.

## What Actually Changes the Math

**Your asset allocation.** The original Trinity Study found that portfolios with 75% stocks outperformed portfolios with 50% stocks over long retirement periods, despite higher volatility. More equities, held through downturns, produced better long-run outcomes. For early retirees with 40+ year horizons, a heavily equity-weighted portfolio — 80 to 90% stocks — is generally supported by the research.

**Sequence of returns.** This is the most underappreciated risk in retirement planning. If markets fall significantly in your first five years of retirement — while you are withdrawing — the damage to your portfolio is much harder to recover from than a crash fifteen years in. A 30% market drop in year two of retirement is far more dangerous than a 30% drop in year eighteen.

This is the strongest argument for keeping 1 to 2 years of expenses in cash or short-term bonds when you first retire — not for the long-term return, but to avoid selling equities at the worst possible time.

**Earning anything at all.** Many people who achieve financial independence continue to earn some income — from part-time consulting, from a passion project, from a small business that requires minimal time. Even $15,000 to $20,000 per year in income dramatically reduces the withdrawal pressure on your portfolio. Barista FIRE — the concept of combining a reduced portfolio with minimal part-time income — works precisely because of this math. A 4% withdrawal on $1,500,000 is $60,000. An additional $20,000 from occasional consulting work means a 2.7% withdrawal rate on the same portfolio. The difference in long-term survival probability is substantial.

## The Number That Changes Everything

I want to tell you something about your financial independence number that took me years to fully understand.

The number is not fixed. It is determined by your spending. And your spending is largely a choice.

Most people approach financial freedom as a fixed target — a large number they must somehow accumulate while their spending stays constant. But the 4% rule makes visible something important: the most powerful move in financial planning is to control spending, not just increase savings.

Every $1,000 you permanently reduce from your annual expenses does two things simultaneously. It raises your monthly savings. And it reduces your financial independence number by $25,000. A household that reduces annual spending from $80,000 to $60,000 does not just save $20,000 this year — it reduces its financial independence target by $500,000 and accelerates its timeline by years.

This is why, in my own life and in every conversation I have had about financial independence, I come back to the same question: what do you actually need to spend to live a life that is genuinely good? Not the life you perform for others. Not the lifestyle that keeps pace with your social circle. The life that actually makes you happy when you are sitting quietly and thinking about it honestly.

That number, for most people, is considerably lower than what they are currently spending.

## The Practical Takeaway

If you are planning for financial independence, here is the framework I use:

**For a standard retirement (age 60+, 30-year horizon):** 4% withdrawal rate. 25x annual expenses.

**For early retirement (age 40-55, 40-year horizon):** 3.5% withdrawal rate. 29x annual expenses.

**For very early retirement (under 40, 50+ year horizon):** 3.25% withdrawal rate. 31x annual expenses. Keep allocation heavily in equities. Maintain genuine spending flexibility.

**In all cases:** diversified, low-cost index funds. An emergency cash buffer of 1-2 years. Spending flexibility that allows you to reduce withdrawals in bad market years. And ideally, some capacity to generate small amounts of income in the early years — even occasional consulting — to reduce withdrawal pressure when portfolio values are most vulnerable.
