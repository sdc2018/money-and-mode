## How AI in Medicine Is Helping Doctors Be Better Doctors

The first thing to understand is this: AI is not replacing doctors. It is helping them do their jobs better, faster, and with fewer mistakes.

Think about an X-ray or a scan. A radiologist — a doctor who reads medical images — might look at hundreds of scans in a single day. By the end of the day, fatigue sets in. Small things can be missed. AI doesn't get tired. Trained on millions of images, it can spot patterns that even experienced eyes might overlook.

In some studies, AI has matched or even outperformed specialists in detecting certain cancers, diabetic eye disease, and heart conditions from scans and images. The doctor still makes the final call. But now they have a very sharp second opinion, available instantly.

This matters enormously in India. We have a massive gap between urban and rural healthcare. A doctor in a small town in Rajasthan or Odisha may not have easy access to a specialist. But with AI-assisted diagnostic tools, that same small-town doctor can get the kind of diagnostic intelligence that a specialist in a Mumbai hospital has. AI is quietly helping to close that gap.

## Drug Discovery, Your Phone, and the AI Health Revolution

This one surprised me when I first learned about it. Developing a new medicine used to take 10 to 15 years and cost billions of dollars. AI is compressing that timeline dramatically — down to 2 to 3 years in some cases.

How? AI can analyse millions of molecular combinations, read every published research paper, and identify which drug candidates are most likely to work — all in the time it would take a human team to review a fraction of that data. During COVID-19, AI tools helped researchers identify vaccine candidates in record time. That's not a small thing.

But here's what's even closer to home.

Your smartphone is becoming a health tool. There are now apps that can analyse a photo of a skin spot and flag whether it looks concerning. Some smartwatches can detect irregular heart rhythms. Apps let you log symptoms over time and notice patterns your doctor might find useful. I'm not saying these replace medical checkups — they absolutely don't. But they give you a head start.

## Understanding Your Own Medical Reports

This is the one that changed things for me personally.

A few months ago, a family member received some blood test results with numbers and abbreviations that meant nothing to us. Normally, you'd wait days for a follow-up appointment to understand what it all meant. Instead, I uploaded the report to Claude (the AI I use daily) and asked it to explain everything in plain English.

Within a minute, I had a clear breakdown — what each marker meant, what the normal range was, why certain values might be elevated, and what questions to ask the doctor. It felt like having a knowledgeable friend I could call at midnight who wouldn't mind walking me through it.

I want to be clear: AI did not diagnose anything. It explained. The doctor still made all medical decisions. But going into that appointment informed, with the right questions ready — that made the conversation so much better.

You can do the same with any medical report, prescription, or health-related document. Upload it to Claude or ChatGPT (AI tools that understand natural language), ask your question in plain words, and get a clear explanation. That knowledge belongs to you.

This is one of the most practical AI health benefits available to anyone, right now, for free.

## Mental Health: A Gentle First Step

Mental health is still stigmatised in many parts of India. Many people who are struggling don't seek help — sometimes because of shame, sometimes because therapy is expensive or not easily available nearby.

AI chatbots are not therapists. Let me say that clearly. If someone is in crisis, they need a real professional.

But for someone who is anxious, overwhelmed, or just needs to talk through their feelings at 2 AM, AI can be a gentle first step. Several apps now offer AI-assisted mental wellness tools — a space to journal, reflect, and get supportive responses. Some people use this as a bridge until they're ready to speak to a real therapist.

It's not perfect. But for someone who has never spoken to anyone about their mental health before, even that small first step matters.
