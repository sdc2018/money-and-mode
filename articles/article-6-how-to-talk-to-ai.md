## The New Employee Analogy (This Will Make It Click)

Imagine you just hired a brilliant new employee. Incredibly smart. Has read thousands of books and reports. Can write, research, calculate, explain — you name it.

But it's their first day. They don't know your business, your clients, your preferences, or your style. They don't know if you want something formal or casual, short or detailed.

Now, if you walk up to them and say "write me an email" — what are they going to produce? Something generic. Maybe even useless.

But if you sit down and say, "I need a polite follow-up email to a client named Rajesh who hasn't replied in two weeks. Keep it short, warm, and professional. My name is Sandip" — now they can actually help you.

AI works exactly the same way. The more context you give, the better the result. This is the entire secret.

## Why Most People Ask AI the Wrong Way

I've been using AI tools almost every day for over two years now. I use Claude — Anthropic's AI — like a smart colleague sitting across from me. I talk to it like a person. I explain what I need, why I need it, and what kind of answer I'm looking for.

Most beginners don't do this. They type one sentence, get a vague answer, and give up.

Here's the honest truth from 25+ years of working with people: communication is everything. Whether you're talking to a junior team member, a consultant, or a machine — the quality of what you ask determines the quality of what you get.

AI has just made this more visible than ever.

## Bad vs. Good: Real Examples You Can Steal

Let me show you exactly what I mean. These are real types of requests — and how a small change makes a massive difference.

**Example 1 — Writing an email**

Bad: *"Write me an email."*

Good: *"Write a polite follow-up email to a client who hasn't replied in 2 weeks. Keep it short and professional. My name is Sandip."*

The second version gives the AI a goal, a tone, a context, and a name. It takes 10 seconds more to type — and saves you 10 minutes of editing a bad draft.

**Example 2 — Summarising something**

Bad: *"Summarise this."*

Good: *"Summarise this 5-page report in 5 bullet points for someone who is not technical. Focus on the key findings and any action items."*

Now the AI knows the length you want, the format (bullets), and who the audience is. Without that, it'll give you a wall of text that's still confusing.

**Example 3 — Getting advice**

Bad: *"How do I save money?"*

Good: *"I earn around ₹60,000 a month, I have two kids in school, and I want to save at least ₹8,000 a month. Give me 5 practical tips that are actually doable for an Indian family."*

The first question will get you generic advice from 1990. The second gets you something relevant to your real life.

**Example 4 — Planning something**

Bad: *"Help me plan a trip."*

Good: *"Help me plan a 4-day trip to Coorg for 2 adults and 1 child. We prefer nature over shopping, our budget is modest, and we'll be driving from Bangalore. Suggest an itinerary."*

You've answered all the questions the AI would have had to guess. Now it can actually plan something useful.

**Example 5 — Understanding a concept**

Bad: *"What is compound interest?"*

Good: *"Explain compound interest to me like I'm 30 years old and just starting to think about investing. Use a simple example with real numbers."*

Now you'll get an explanation that meets you where you are — not a textbook definition.

## The One Habit That Changes Everything

Here's what I do every single time I use AI: I tell it who I am, what I need, and what I'll do with the answer.

That's it. Three things.

For example: *"I'm a small business owner in India. I need a simple WhatsApp message to send to my customers announcing a 10% discount this week. Keep it friendly and under 50 words."*

When I started doing this consistently, the results improved dramatically. I stopped getting generic answers. I started getting things I could actually use immediately.

You don't need any technical knowledge to do this. You don't need to understand how AI works under the hood. You just need to talk to it like you're briefing a very capable, very literal-minded assistant.

You already have the foundations. AI just gives you a new place to use them.
